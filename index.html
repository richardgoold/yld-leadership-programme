01
Finance 101
How money flows through a consulting business

Outcome

Every leader can read a P&L, understand how their decisions affect margin and cash, and ask the right financial questions with confidence.

The Shift We’re Engineering

Walking in

“Finance is the CFO’s job. I focus on delivery.”

→
Walking out

“Every decision I make has a P&L consequence — and I can see it.”

Session Storyboard

90–120 minutes • Frame → Apply → Reflect → Commit

OPEN 10 min
The Provocation

Open cold with a scenario, not a slide. Read this to the room:

“It’s Monday morning. Your biggest client has just paused their project — effective immediately. That’s £180k of revenue gone from this quarter. You have three contractors on that engagement who are now unbillable. Your utilisation just dropped from 86% to 71%. Meanwhile, a new prospect wants to start in two weeks but you’d need to hire two senior engineers to staff it — and the deal hasn’t signed yet. What do you do?”

Don’t answer it. Don’t discuss it yet. Just let it sit. Say: “By the end of this session, you’ll be able to work through that scenario fluently. Right now, most of you can’t — and that’s exactly why we’re here.”

⚠ Facilitation note: This will create discomfort. That’s the point. Resist the urge to reassure. The discomfort is the motivation for the next 90 minutes.

FRAME 30 min
Teaching: How Money Flows Through YLD

Walk through the consulting P&L using YLD’s actual structure. Use the interactive simulator on screen as the visual anchor — move the sliders as you teach each concept.

Topic 1 • 8 min
Revenue Mechanics

Start with the top line. Revenue = billable heads × billable days × day rate. Simple formula, but each variable hides complexity. Explain realised rate vs list rate (discounts, write-offs, fixed-price overruns). Explain revenue recognition — when does the work you did last month actually become revenue? Cover the sales-to-delivery lag: a deal signed today doesn’t generate revenue for 4–6 weeks.

YLD anchor: Show the simulator with 70 heads at 86% utilisation and £850 day rate. “This is roughly where we are today.”

Topic 2 • 8 min
Cost Structure & Margin

Two types of cost: direct (COGS) and overheads. COGS = the people doing the work (salaries, contractor costs). Overheads = everything else (rent, sales, management, tools). Gross profit = revenue minus COGS. EBITDA = gross profit minus overheads. This is the number that tells you whether the business is actually making money.

YLD anchor: Move the cost-per-head slider. “Watch what happens to EBITDA when average cost goes from £55k to £70k. That’s the difference between hiring a mid-level and a senior engineer.”

Topic 3 • 7 min
Utilisation & Leverage

These are the two margin drivers in a people business. Utilisation = % of available time that’s billable. YLD runs at 86% vs a 76% sector average — that’s exceptional but fragile. Every point of utilisation is worth roughly £130k in revenue. Leverage = ratio of junior to senior staff on engagements. Higher leverage = higher margin (if quality holds).

YLD anchor: Drop utilisation from 86% to 76% on the simulator. “That’s what ‘matching the industry average’ actually costs us.”

Topic 4 • 7 min
Cash vs Profit

A profitable business can run out of cash. WIP (work done but not yet invoiced), debtors (invoiced but not yet paid), and DSO (days sales outstanding) explain the gap. If you deliver £100k of work in January, invoice in February, and get paid in April — that’s three months of carrying cost. For a growing business, this cash gap gets bigger, not smaller.

Question to the room: “If YLD grew revenue 30% next year, would we need more or less cash? Why?” (Answer: more — growth eats cash before it generates profit.)

⚠ Facilitation note: Watch for glazed eyes during cost structure. If energy drops, pause and ask: “Quick show of hands — who has ever approved a scope change without thinking about what it does to utilisation?” Every hand should go up. That’s your bridge back to relevance.

APPLY 35 min
The YLD P&L Challenge

Split into groups of 3–4. Each group gets the same starting P&L (based on real YLD numbers) and must respond to three interconnected events that arrive in sequence.

Event 1

Your biggest client pauses their project. £180k/quarter revenue disappears. Three contractors are now unbillable.

Event 2

A contractor on another engagement has overrun by 3 weeks. The client won’t pay for the extra time. That’s £45k of unrecoverable cost.

Event 3

A new prospect wants to start in 2 weeks. It’s a £300k engagement but you’d need to hire 2 senior engineers. The deal hasn’t signed yet.

How to run it

Groups get 20 minutes to decide: What do you do about each event? What are the financial consequences of your decisions? Use the simulator to model your choices. Then each group presents their decisions in 3 minutes — followed by challenge from the room.

What good looks like

Groups that ask: “What does this do to EBITDA?” before deciding. Groups that quantify trade-offs rather than just describing them. Groups that spot the cash vs profit distinction (hiring ahead of a deal that hasn’t signed is a cash bet, not just a P&L decision).

⚠ Facilitation note: The exercise is designed to create disagreement within groups. Some will want to cut contractors immediately; others will want to hold them for the new prospect. Neither answer is wrong — the learning is in articulating the financial reasoning. If all groups agree, the exercise hasn’t worked hard enough.

REFLECT 10 min
Facilitated Debrief

Don’t just review the answers. Focus the debrief on three questions:

1. What surprised you? Most people are surprised by how quickly small decisions compound. A 3-week overrun feels manageable until you see it on the P&L next to the paused project.

2. Where did your group disagree? The disagreements reveal different mental models. Some people optimise for revenue, some for margin, some for risk. Name the difference.

3. What would you do differently on Monday? Ground the learning in their actual role. “What decision do you have coming up this month that now looks different?”

⚠ Facilitation note: Let silence sit after each question. Count to 7 before you speak. The best insights come after the obvious answers have been given.

COMMIT 5 min
Personal Commitment

Each person writes down one thing they will do differently in the next two weeks. Not “learn more about finance” — something specific and observable. Examples:

“Before approving my next scope change, I’ll calculate the utilisation impact.”
“I’ll ask the finance team to walk me through last month’s actual P&L.”
“I’ll check the DSO on my two biggest clients and chase if overdue.”
“Next time I propose hiring, I’ll include a payback calculation.”

Share with the person sitting next to you. They’re your accountability partner — they’ll ask you about it at the start of the next session.

CLOSE 5 min
Return to the Opening Scenario

Re-read the opening scenario. Ask: “Who can now talk through what they’d do — with numbers?” The difference between how the room responds now vs 90 minutes ago is the evidence that the session worked.

Close with the takeaway: “Financial curiosity is more valuable than financial jargon. From today, always ask: how does this affect EBITDA or cash?”

02
Managing Client Relationships
From delivery partner to trusted adviser

Outcome

Every leader sees themselves as a relationship manager and knows how to expand accounts, handle difficult conversations, and generate referrals.

The Shift We’re Engineering

Walking in

“I’m a delivery lead. Sales is someone else’s job.”

→
Walking out

“Every conversation I have with a client is either building or eroding our future pipeline.”

Session Storyboard

90–120 minutes • Frame → Apply → Reflect → Commit

OPEN 10 min
The Provocation

“Your biggest client’s sponsor — the person who championed YLD internally, who fought for budget renewals, who referred you to two other companies — just emailed to say they’re leaving. Their last day is Friday. You have no other senior relationship at that account. The contract renewal is in 6 weeks. What do you do in the next 48 hours?”

Let the room feel the gap. Most people won’t have a clear plan. That discomfort is the motivation for the session. Say: “If you can’t answer that fluently, you have a single point of failure in your most important client relationship. Let’s fix that.”

FRAME 25 min
Teaching: From Delivery Partner to Trusted Adviser
Topic 1 • 8 min
The Relationship Spectrum

Three levels: vendor (you do what you’re told), delivery partner (you’re trusted to deliver well), trusted adviser (you’re asked for opinions on things outside your scope). Most YLD relationships sit at level 2. The revenue difference between 2 and 3 is enormous — trusted advisers get called first for new work.

Exercise: Ask each person to privately place their top 3 client relationships on the spectrum. Don’t share yet — this becomes the baseline for the later exercise.

Topic 2 • 8 min
The NewDay Playbook

NewDay grew from 6 engineers to 31. Average monthly revenue per customer grew 139% (£54k to £128k). Top 5 clients stay 30 months. This didn’t happen by accident — it happened because delivery quality created trust, trust created conversation, and conversation created expansion.

YLD anchor: “Every one of you is sitting on a version of this playbook. The question is whether you’re running it deliberately or leaving it to chance.”

Topic 3 • 9 min
Difficult Conversations & Commercial Listening

Scope creep, quality concerns, rate negotiations, contract renewals. These aren’t problems to avoid — they’re the moments where trust is either built or broken. Commercial listening means hearing what the client says but understanding the context behind it: budget cycles, internal politics, competing priorities.

⚠ Facilitation note: “Sales is not a dirty word” is a key reframe. If anyone pushes back (“I’m an engineer, not a salesperson”), redirect: “You’re not selling. You’re helping clients solve problems they haven’t fully articulated yet. That’s what trusted advisers do.”

APPLY 40 min
The Client Scenario Room

Split into pairs. Each pair gets one scenario. One person plays the YLD lead, the other plays the client. Role-play for 8 minutes, then swap roles. Debrief in the full group.

Scenario A

The client is unhappy with a senior engineer’s performance. They want them replaced. Your engineer doesn’t know yet.

Scenario B

The client wants to extend scope significantly but says there’s no additional budget. They expect you to absorb it.

Scenario C

A former client contact has moved to a new company and reaches out. They loved working with YLD but their new company has never heard of you.

What good looks like

Scenario A: The YLD lead doesn’t immediately agree to replace. They ask what specifically isn’t working, propose a recovery plan, and protect their engineer’s dignity. Scenario B: They don’t say no, but they don’t say yes either — they reframe around priorities and trade-offs. Scenario C: They don’t just catch up socially — they ask about the new company’s challenges and plant seeds.

⚠ Facilitation note: The role-play will feel awkward at first. That’s fine. After 2 minutes most people forget they’re acting. The client-side player is crucial — brief them to be realistic, not easy. If someone playing the client is too compliant, the exercise loses its edge.

REFLECT 10 min
Facilitated Debrief

1. What was harder — being YLD or being the client? Usually people find playing the client more revealing. They suddenly see what it feels like to be managed rather than advised.

2. Where did you default to “fixing” instead of listening? Engineers especially jump to solutions. The skill is sitting in the client’s frustration long enough to understand the real issue.

3. Which of your real client relationships needs attention right now?

COMMIT 5 min
Personal Commitment
“I’ll schedule a non-project catch-up with my top client contact this fortnight.”
“I’ll map all relationships at my biggest account — not just my day-to-day contact.”

Leading People
From managing tasks to growing people

Outcome

Leaders shift from “doing the work” to creating the conditions for their teams to thrive — across London, Lisbon, and Porto.

The Shift We’re Engineering

Walking in

“I’m the best engineer on my team. That’s why I lead it.”

→
Walking out

“My job is to create the conditions for my team to do their best work — not to do it for them.”

Session Storyboard

90–120 minutes • Frame → Apply → Reflect → Commit

OPEN 10 min
The Provocation

“Your best engineer just resigned. They told HR they love the work but don’t feel like they’re growing. You had no idea. Their last 1:1 with you was three months ago. Your team in Porto heard the news before you did. What does this tell you about how you’re leading?”

This scenario will hit home for most people. Don’t let them externalise (“HR should have flagged it”). The point is: if you didn’t see it coming, the feedback loop isn’t working.

FRAME 25 min
Teaching: The Leadership Shift
From “I deliver” to “I create conditions”

Most YLD leaders were promoted because they were excellent practitioners. The trap is continuing to do the thing you were great at instead of doing the new thing: setting context, making space, growing people. Delegation is not abdication — it’s giving ownership while staying close enough to support.

Feedback as a Habit, Not an Event

If someone only hears how they’re doing twice a year, you’ve lost 350 days of potential course correction. The goal is to make feedback so normal that it doesn’t require courage to give or receive.

Remote & Distributed Leadership

YLD operates across London, Lisbon, and Porto. Leading people you can’t see requires deliberate over-communication, structured touchpoints, and trust that was earned before the distance.

⚠ Facilitation note: This is the session where egos are most at risk. People who see themselves as strong leaders may resist the idea that they have gaps. Keep the tone curious, not critical. The Leadership Mirror exercise will do the heavy lifting.

APPLY 40 min
The Leadership Mirror

Each person self-assesses against five leadership dimensions (1–5 scale). Then they receive anonymous input from two peers in the room on the same dimensions.

Context-Setting
Does your team know why, not just what?
Delegation
Ownership given, not micromanaged?
Feedback
Regular, honest, two-way?
Decision-Making
Clear, timely, inclusive enough?
Team Growth
Are people developing under you?
How to run it

Step 1 (5 min): Self-assess. Step 2 (10 min): Two peers score you anonymously on printed cards. Step 3 (5 min): Read your peer scores privately. Step 4 (20 min): Facilitated discussion — not “who scored what” but “where was the biggest gap between how you see yourself and how others see you?”

⚠ Facilitation note: The gap between self and peer perception is the entire point. Some people will score themselves low; their peers will score them higher. That’s also insight. Protect anonymity fiercely — if anyone tries to identify who scored them, shut it down immediately.

COMMIT 10 min
Personal Commitment

Based on your mirror results, write one specific thing you’ll change about how you lead in the next two weeks.

“I’ll have a 20-minute 1:1 with each direct report this fortnight and ask what’s blocking them.”
“I’ll delegate the next client demo entirely and only give feedback afterwards.”

04
Risk, Judgement & Accountability
Owning decisions with incomplete information

Outcome

Risk identification and escalation becomes a normal leadership habit, not something reserved for the CEO.

The Shift We’re Engineering

Walking in

“I’ll flag it if it becomes a real problem.”

→
Walking out

“I surface risks early with options, not just issues. That’s leadership, not weakness.”

Session Storyboard

90–120 minutes • Frame → Apply → Reflect → Commit

OPEN 10 min
The Provocation

“A project has been drifting for three weeks. The client’s stakeholder has gone quiet. Your lead engineer mentioned last month that the requirements were unclear but ‘it’ll be fine.’ You didn’t follow up. Now the client has escalated directly to the CEO. How did we get here?”

Every person in the room will recognise some version of this. The pattern is always the same: a small signal was ignored, nobody escalated, and by the time it surfaced it was a crisis. Say: “The question isn’t why the project went wrong. It’s why nobody said anything three weeks ago.”

FRAME 20 min
Teaching: Risk, Judgement & Escalation
The 70% Rule

Act when you have 70% of the information you’d like. Waiting for 100% means you’re always late. The skill is recognising when you have enough to decide and when you genuinely need more.

Escalation Without Abdication

Raising a risk early is not passing the problem. Come with options, not just issues. “I’ve identified X. Here are three things we could do. I recommend Y because...”

Three Types of Risk at YLD

Client delivery risk: scope drift, stakeholder disengagement, team fatigue. Commercial risk: revenue concentration, contractor dependency, pricing pressure. People risk: attrition, single points of failure, knowledge silos.

APPLY 35 min
The Pre-Mortem

“It’s January 2028. YLD has missed its growth targets badly. The business has stalled. Revenue is flat, key people have left, and two major clients didn’t renew. What went wrong?”

In groups, work backwards from this failure. Identify the decisions, the missed signals, and the risks that nobody owned. Then map each risk to a current owner and agree early warning indicators.

What good looks like

Groups that go beyond the obvious (“we lost a big client”) to the structural (“we had no succession plan for key relationships” or “we never resolved the tension between growth and quality”). The best pre-mortems surface the things nobody is currently saying.

⚠ Facilitation note: This is the most emotionally powerful exercise in the programme. Give it space. Don’t rush the debrief. The risk map that comes out of this should be photographed and followed up — it’s a genuine strategic artefact.

COMMIT 5 min
Personal Commitment
“I’ll raise the project risk I’ve been sitting on in this week’s leadership meeting.”
“I’ll adopt the ‘options not issues’ format for every escalation I make.”

“Asking for support is not a sign of weakness. It is an expected part of working as a strong leadership team.”

05
Trust at Scale
Building and protecting trust as you grow

Outcome

The team can diagnose trust gaps — internal and external — and design specific interventions to strengthen them.

The Shift We’re Engineering

Walking in

“Trust is a soft skill. We just need to deliver well.”

→
Walking out

“Trust is measurable, and I can diagnose exactly where it’s strong and where it’s at risk.”

Session Storyboard

90–120 minutes • Frame → Apply → Reflect → Commit

OPEN 5 min
The Provocation

“Think of the last time a client renewed without you having to sell anything. That happened because of trust. Now think of the last time you hesitated before raising a problem with a colleague. That also happened because of trust — or the lack of it.”

FRAME 25 min
Teaching: The Trust Equation

Trust = Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy ÷ Self-Orientation

(Maister, Green & Galford)

Walk through each element with YLD examples. Credibility: do clients believe you know what you’re talking about? Reliability: do you do what you said you’d do, every time? Intimacy: does the client feel safe sharing real problems with you? Self-orientation (the denominator): do you care more about their outcome or yours?

YLD anchor: “YLD’s 30-month average client tenure exists because all four elements are working. But trust is fragile — one missed denominator spike can undo years of numerator building.”

Internal Trust & Psychological Safety

Trust between leadership team members. Trust between London, Lisbon, and Porto. Trust between the team and the Board. If problems don’t surface early internally, they surface late externally — with clients and in crises.

APPLY 35 min
Trust Mapping

Each person maps the key trust relationships in their world — team, peers, clients, Board — and honestly assesses each on a 1–5 scale using the trust equation elements. Then identify the one relationship where trust is most at risk and design a specific intervention.

Important: Interventions are shared in pairs, not with the full group. Some of the most at-risk relationships will be internal, possibly with people in the room. Pairs provide safety. Full-group sharing would shut people down.

⚠ Facilitation note: Watch for people who only map client relationships and avoid internal ones. Gently prompt: “What about the relationships in this room? Where’s trust strong between you and your peers, and where does it need work?”

COMMIT 5 min
Personal Commitment
“I’ll have the conversation I’ve been avoiding with [peer] about how we work together.”
“I’ll proactively share a mistake with my client this month instead of burying it.”

“Trust scales through consistency, not heroics. How you behave when things go wrong tells people more than anything you do when things go right.”

Prioritisation, trade-offs, and sector expansion

Outcome

The Shift We’re Engineering

Walking in

“We should do more. We need to be everywhere.”

→
Walking out

Session Storyboard

90–120 minutes • Frame → Apply → Reflect → Commit

OPEN 5 min
The Provocation

“You have the capacity to pursue three new opportunities this quarter. There are seven on the table. Which four do you say no to — and how do you explain that to the people who brought them in?”

FRAME 20 min
Teaching: The Prioritisation Framework
Opportunity Cost

Every ‘yes’ is a ‘no’ to something else. In a resource-constrained business, this is the most important concept. The cost of doing something isn’t just the budget — it’s what you couldn’t do instead.

Four Lenses for Every Decision
Impact
How much does this move the needle?
Effort
What does it cost in time and people?
Alignment
Does it fit where YLD is heading?
Reversibility
Can we undo it if we’re wrong?
APPLY 45 min
Sector Expansion Sprint + AI Accelerator

Teams choose a new sector for YLD to explore. Using AI tools live in the session, they have 45 minutes to:

Research: Map pain points, key buyers, competitive landscape
Draft: Create a front door statement for this sector
Target: Identify 5 specific companies and an entry hypothesis
Build: Create a reusable prompt library for the team

Each team presents their sector thesis in 5 minutes. The room challenges: “Would you bet £100k of YLD’s time on this?” This forces rigour beyond the AI output.

⚠ Facilitation note: Ensure laptops/phones are available for AI use. Have backup prompts ready in case teams get stuck. The output quality matters less than the process — the insight is that AI dramatically accelerates research but doesn’t replace judgement.

COMMIT 5 min
Personal Commitment
“I’ll apply the 4-lens framework to the next opportunity that lands on my desk before saying yes.”
“I’ll use the prompt library to research one potential expansion area and bring findings to the team.”

“Prioritisation is not about doing less. It’s about doing fewer things better, and being honest about what you’re choosing not to do.”

“I run my area well. That’s my job.”

→

Session Storyboard

90–120 minutes • Frame → Apply → Reflect → Commit

OPEN 10 min
The Provocation

“The Board asks each of you to present a 5-minute summary of YLD’s biggest challenge right now — not in your area, in the whole business. You have 60 seconds to think. Go.”

Cold-call 3–4 people. Most will default to their own function. That’s the gap. Leaders of a business think about the whole, not just their piece. Say: “If you can only see the business through the lens of your function, you’re not leading the business yet.”

FRAME 15 min
Teaching: The Systems View

Engineering decisions affect commercial outcomes. Sales promises create delivery pressure. People decisions shape the cost base. Hiring choices affect client quality. Every function is connected. Leaders of a business see the ripples, not just the splash.

Board-Ready Thinking

A good board paper presents a decision, not an update. Structure: problem, context, options with trade-offs, recommendation, risks. If your paper ends with “for discussion” instead of “we recommend X because...”, you’re not leading yet.

The Rhythm of the Business

Weekly, monthly, quarterly cadences that create visibility and accountability without bureaucracy. The difference between an information-sharing meeting and a decision-making meeting. Which one are yours?

APPLY 45 min
The Board Pack Simulation

Each group is given a real (anonymised) business challenge facing YLD. You have 30 minutes to prepare a one-page board paper: problem, options, recommendation, risks. Then present to the room — who play the role of the Board.

Example Challenge A

YLD’s top 3 clients represent 55% of revenue. One is showing signs of reducing spend. What do you recommend?

Example Challenge B

Three senior engineers are being recruited by competitors. Matching salaries would increase cost base by £180k. What do you recommend?

What good looks like

Papers that present a clear recommendation with financial reasoning, not just a description of the problem. Presentations that invite challenge rather than defending a position. Teams that disagree internally and resolve it before presenting.

⚠ Facilitation note: When playing the Board, be constructively tough. Ask “What’s the cost of doing nothing?” and “How does this affect EBITDA?” (connecting back to Theme 1). This exercise is the capstone — it should feel like a real board meeting.

COMMIT 5 min
Personal Commitment
“I’ll attend one meeting outside my function this month and contribute.”
“Next time I present upwards, I’ll use the decision-not-update format.”

“You are not just the head of your function. You are a leader of this business. Act accordingly.”

04 — Harnessing AI

AI as a Working Tool, Not a Talking Point

We won’t talk about AI in the abstract. We’ll use it — live, in the session — across three themes that matter to YLD’s growth. Every participant will leave with practical skills and a prompt library they can use immediately.

⚡

Optimise What We Do

Use AI to sharpen sector research, validate market hypotheses, and identify opportunities faster. Turn a week of desk research into 30 minutes of focused prompting.

  • Sector mapping and competitor analysis
  • Identifying digital transformation pain points by vertical
  • Building a qualified target list with entry-point hypotheses
🔧

Be More Efficient

AI for drafting proposals, synthesising feedback, preparing board papers, and creating first-cut messaging. Accelerating the path from thinking to output.

  • Proposal and SOW drafting from brief notes
  • Summarising meeting notes into actions and decisions
  • Building templates for recurring communications
🚀

Evolve the Client Offer

Position YLD as AI-literate. Understand how clients’ needs are shifting, where AI creates new service lines, and how to have credible conversations about AI-enabled transformation.

  • How client buying patterns are changing
  • Where AI creates new service lines for YLD
  • Framing YLD’s AI capability in client conversations

What We’ll Actually Do in the Session

How to Run Each Exercise

Sector Expansion Sprint (45 min)

Groups of 3–4. Give them 5 minutes to choose a sector, then 30 minutes to research, draft, and build using AI. Final 10 minutes: each group pitches their sector thesis in 3 minutes. Room challenges with: “Would you bet £100k of YLD’s time on this?” The challenge question forces rigour — AI makes research easy, but judgement is still human.

Efficiency Challenge (20 min)

Individual exercise. Each person works on their own task with AI. Pair up after 10 minutes to share what worked and refine prompts together. The output is a reusable prompt they keep. Have 2–3 people demo their before/after to the room — the efficiency gains are usually dramatic and create immediate buy-in.

Client Conversation Role-Play (20 min)

Pairs. The scenario: “Your client asks how YLD uses AI.” Step 1: Use AI to research the client’s sector challenges in 5 minutes. Step 2: Draft a credible, specific answer (not “we use AI for lots of things”). Step 3: Role-play the conversation. The insight is that a generic answer sounds hollow, but a sector-specific one sounds like expertise.

Prompt Library (ongoing)

Throughout all exercises, have people save their best prompts to a shared doc. At the end, spend 5 minutes curating: which prompts worked best? Which are generic vs YLD-specific? The library should be immediately usable after the session, not aspirational.

⚠ Common pitfalls: (1) People get distracted exploring AI capabilities instead of completing the exercise — set tight time boundaries and keep them focused on the output, not the tool. (2) Some participants will resist (“I don’t need AI”) — pair them with an enthusiast and let the results speak. (3) Don’t let the session become a demo of one person’s AI skills — everyone must do, not watch.

05 — The Front Door

One Sentence That Opens Doors

We’ll build a front door statement together — one sentence that gets YLD into the right conversations quickly. Not a strategy rewrite. A practical tool everyone can use.

In plain English — what situation are clients typically in when they call YLD?

02

03

What is YLD’s real differentiator — not what we wish it was, but what clients actually value?

04

The Mistaken Identity

What do we get mistaken for today? What does that do to win rates and pricing?

05

Three Proof Points

Which three facts — with numbers — do we always lead with?

What we’ll produce

→ One sentence that gets YLD into the right conversations

→ A 30-second version for when you have more time

→ Three proof points with numbers

The Critical Framing

The front door statement is not a tagline, a mission statement, or a list of services. It follows a specific format:

“When [client situation], YLD [what we do].”

This is what we do when X is happening. It’s situational, not descriptive. It tells someone what problem to bring to YLD, not what YLD is. The difference matters: a capability list (“we do engineering and design”) invites comparison. A situation statement (“when your platform can’t scale fast enough for your growth”) invites conversation.

☑ Facilitator Brief: Running the Front Door Exercise

How to Run It (45–60 min)

Step 1: Individual Drafting (10 min)

Each person writes their own version of the front door statement using the “When [situation], YLD [what we do]” format. Silently. No discussion. This surfaces the range of how people currently describe the business — which is usually wildly inconsistent.

Step 2: Share & Cluster (10 min)

Read them all out. Write them on a board or screen. Don’t critique yet. Just notice: where is there overlap? Where is there contradiction? The inconsistency itself is the first insight.

Step 3: Work the Five Questions (15 min)

Walk through the five steps (Situation, 10-Second Test, Genuine Edge, Mistaken Identity, Proof Points) as a full group. The “Mistaken Identity” question is the most revealing — push hard here. If YLD gets mistaken for a body shop, that kills pricing. If it gets mistaken for a big consultancy, that kills intimacy. Name it.

Step 4: Converge (10 min)

Draft a single statement together. It will not be perfect in the room — that’s fine. The goal is alignment on the shape, not final copy. The refinement happens afterwards. But the core framing — “when X is happening” — must survive.

Step 5: Pressure Test (5–10 min)

Role-play it: one person meets someone at a conference. “What does your company do?” Use the statement. Does it land? Does the other person ask a follow-up question or glaze over? If it works, the other person says “oh interesting, tell me more.” If it doesn’t, they say “ah, so like Accenture?”

The Pushback You’ll Get (and How to Handle It)

“But we do lots of things”

Yes, and nobody remembers a list. One sentence gets you into the conversation. The rest comes later.

“This is too narrow”

Narrow is the point. A front door is one door, not a car park. You can have different doors for different sectors.

“Our clients don’t all have the same situation”

Correct. Start with the most common situation. You can build sector-specific versions later. But you need one that works universally first.

“This feels like marketing’s job”

Marketing writes copy. Leaders define positioning. If the people delivering the work can’t articulate what YLD does in one sentence, the positioning isn’t real.

⚠ Facilitation note: Protect the “when X is happening” format fiercely. The room will naturally drift towards a capability statement (“we are a...”) instead of a situational one (“when your...”). Every time it drifts, pull it back: “That tells me what you are. What situation is the client in when they need you?”
